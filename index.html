Born-Haber Cycles

Key Concept: Born-Haber cycles use Hess's Law to calculate lattice enthalpies that cannot be measured directly.

Essential Definitions

Lattice Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°ₗₐₜₜ)
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions under standard conditions.
Sign: ALWAYS NEGATIVE (exothermic)
Example: Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) → NaCl(s)
AQA mark scheme note: Must include state symbols and "1 mole"
Lattice Enthalpy of Dissociation
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of an ionic compound is completely dissociated into gaseous ions.
Sign: ALWAYS POSITIVE (endothermic)
Example: NaCl(s) → Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g)
Note: This is the reverse of lattice formation, so opposite sign
Standard Enthalpy of Atomisation (ΔH°ₐₜ)
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of gaseous atoms is formed from the element in its standard state.
Sign: ALWAYS POSITIVE (endothermic)
Example: ½Cl₂(g) → Cl(g) or Na(s) → Na(g)
AQA note: Per mole of gaseous atoms formed
First Ionisation Energy (IE₁)
The enthalpy change required to remove one electron from each atom in 1 mole of gaseous atoms to form 1 mole of gaseous 1+ ions.
Sign: ALWAYS POSITIVE
Example: Na(g) → Na⁺(g) + e⁻
Must show the electron in the equation
First Electron Affinity (EA₁)
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of gaseous atoms gains 1 electron per atom to form 1 mole of gaseous 1- ions.
Sign: Usually NEGATIVE (exothermic for most elements)
Example: Cl(g) + e⁻ → Cl⁻(g)
Note: Second electron affinity is always positive

Drawing Born-Haber Cycles - Step by Step Method

1START with elements in standard states (left side, middle of page)
2DIRECT ROUTE (one arrow) to the compound (usually down for ΔH°f)
3BUILD UPWARDS to gaseous atoms
4IONISE to get gaseous ions (keep going up)
5COME DOWN to the compound (lattice formation)
Common Mistakes:
• Forgetting electrons in ionisation equations
• Confusing lattice formation (negative) with dissociation (positive)
• Not doubling values for compounds like MgCl₂ or CaF₂
• Forgetting both IE₁ and IE₂ for Group 2 compounds
Worked Example 1: Born-Haber Calculation for NaBr
Given Data:
• ΔH°f(NaBr) = -360 kJ mol⁻¹
• Atomisation Na = +109 kJ mol⁻¹
• Atomisation Br = +112 kJ mol⁻¹ (½Br₂(l) → Br(g))
• IE₁(Na) = +494 kJ mol⁻¹
• EA₁(Br) = -325 kJ mol⁻¹
Find: Lattice formation enthalpy

Solution:
ΔH°f = ΔH°at(Na) + ΔH°at(Br) + IE₁(Na) + EA₁(Br) + ΔH°latt
Substituting values with correct signs:
-360 = (+109) + (+112) + (+494) + (-325) + ΔH°latt
-360 = +390 + ΔH°latt
ΔH°latt = -360 - 390
ΔH°latt = -750 kJ mol⁻¹
✓ Calculation verified: Negative value confirms exothermic lattice formation
Worked Example 2: Born-Haber for MgCl₂
Watch for these multipliers:
• Atomisation of Cl: × 2 (need 2 Cl atoms)
• Electron affinity of Cl: × 2 (form 2 Cl⁻ ions)
• Second ionisation energy needed for Mg²⁺

Calculation:
ΔH°f(MgCl₂) = -641 kJ mol⁻¹
Sum of steps = +148 + (2×121) + 738 + 1451 + (2×-364) + ΔH°latt
-641 = +148 + 242 + 738 + 1451 - 728 + ΔH°latt
-641 = +1851 + ΔH°latt
ΔH°latt = -2492 kJ mol⁻¹

Enthalpy of Solution

Standard Enthalpy of Solution (ΔH°sol)
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of an ionic solid dissolves in water to give a solution of infinite dilution.
Sign: Can be positive OR negative
Example: NaCl(s) + aq → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
Standard Enthalpy of Hydration (ΔH°hyd)
The enthalpy change when 1 mole of gaseous ions dissolves in water to give a solution of infinite dilution.
Sign: ALWAYS NEGATIVE (exothermic)
Example: Na⁺(g) + aq → Na⁺(aq)
ΔH°sol = -ΔH°latt(formation) + ΣΔH°hyd(ions)
OR
ΔH°sol = +ΔH°latt(dissociation) + ΣΔH°hyd(ions)
Factors affecting magnitudes:
• Smaller ions → More exothermic lattice & hydration enthalpies
• Higher charge → More exothermic lattice & hydration enthalpies
• If |ΔH°hyd| > |ΔH°latt| → Exothermic dissolution (usually soluble)
• If |ΔH°latt| > |ΔH°hyd| → Endothermic dissolution (may be insoluble)

Entropy & Gibbs Free Energy

Entropy (S)
A measure of the dispersal of energy in a system (disorder)
Units: J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ (note: NOT kJ)
Key Point: S values are ALWAYS POSITIVE (there's always some energy dispersal)
Standard entropy (S°): Entropy of 1 mole under standard conditions

When Does Entropy Increase?

✅ Entropy INCREASES when:
• State changes: solid → liquid → gas
• More particles produced (especially gases)
• Temperature increases
• Dissolving occurs

The Gibbs Equation

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

For feasibility: ΔG ≤ 0
Note: Feasibility means thermodynamically possible, NOT that reaction will occur
CRITICAL Unit Conversion:
• ΔH is in kJ mol⁻¹
• ΔS is in J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
• MUST convert: ΔS(kJ) = ΔS(J) ÷ 1000
• Temperature MUST be in KELVIN (K = °C + 273)
AQA Mark Scheme Point:
A reaction may be thermodynamically feasible (ΔG < 0) but not occur due to:
• High activation energy (kinetic barrier)
• Very slow rate at the given temperature
Always distinguish between thermodynamic feasibility and kinetic factors!

Feasibility Quick Reference

ΔH ΔS TΔS ΔG Feasibility
-ve +ve +ve Always -ve Always feasible ✓
-ve -ve -ve -ve at low T Feasible at LOW T
+ve +ve +ve -ve at high T Feasible at HIGH T
+ve -ve -ve Always +ve Never feasible ✗
Worked Example: Minimum Temperature for Feasibility
Reaction: ZnCO₃(s) → ZnO(s) + CO₂(g)
Given: ΔH = +71 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔS = +175.2 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
Find: Minimum temperature for feasibility

Solution:
At the boundary of feasibility, ΔG = 0
0 = ΔH - TΔS
T = ΔH/ΔS

Convert units: ΔS = 175.2 ÷ 1000 = 0.1752 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
T = 71 ÷ 0.1752 = 405.3 K

Answer: Reaction becomes feasible at T > 405 K (132°C)
✓ AQA mark point: State "feasible when T > [value]" not "at T = [value]"

Rate Equations

Collision Theory & Activation Energy

Rate of Reaction
The change in concentration of a substance in a given time.
Units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
Activation Energy (Ea)
The minimum energy required to start a reaction by the breaking of bonds.
Key Point: Only collisions with E ≥ Ea lead to successful reactions

Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

Factor Effect on Rate Explanation
↑ Concentration Increases More particles in same volume → more successful collisions per unit time
↑ Pressure (gases) Increases More particles in same volume → more successful collisions per unit time
↑ Surface Area Increases More space for collisions → more successful collisions per unit time
↑ Temperature Increases Particles have more energy → higher proportion with E ≥ Ea
Add Catalyst Increases Lowers activation energy → higher proportion of collisions successful

The Rate Equation

Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ

Understanding Orders

Order Relationship If [A] doubles... Graph Shape
0 Rate independent of [A] Rate unchanged Horizontal line
1 Rate ∝ [A] Rate × 2 Straight line
2 Rate ∝ [A]² Rate × 4 Upward curve

Methods for Determining Orders

1. Initial Rates Method

1Find two experiments where only ONE concentration changes
2Calculate the concentration change factor
3Calculate the rate change factor
4Use: Rate factor = (Conc factor)^order

2. Continuous Monitoring Method

A method where concentration is measured at regular time intervals during the reaction, allowing determination of order from the concentration-time graph shape.
Example: Bromine + Methanoic Acid
Reaction: Br₂ + HCOOH → 2HBr + CO₂ (catalysed by H⁺)

The reaction is followed using a colorimeter as Br₂ is coloured.
Using excess methanoic acid ensures [HCOOH] remains virtually constant.

Key observations from concentration-time graph:
• Measure half-lives at different points
• If t₁/₂ constant → First order
• If t₁/₂ increases as concentration decreases → Second order
• If concentration decreases linearly → Zero order

3. Half-Life Method

Half-Life (t₁/₂)
The time taken for the concentration of a reactant to halve.
Order Half-life behavior Graph shape ([A] vs time)
0 Decreases as concentration decreases Straight line (linear decrease)
1 Constant (independent of concentration) Exponential decay curve
2 Increases as concentration decreases Steep initial decline, then leveling
For first-order reactions:
t₁/₂ = ln(2)/k = 0.693/k
This is why radioactive decay (always first order) has a constant half-life!
Worked Example: Determining Orders
Data for reaction: A + B → C + D

Experiment [A] / mol dm⁻³ [B] / mol dm⁻³ Initial Rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
1 0.10 0.10 2.0 × 10⁻⁴
2 0.20 0.10 4.0 × 10⁻⁴
3 0.10 0.20 8.0 × 10⁻⁴

Solution:
Finding order with respect to A:
Compare experiments 1 and 2 (B constant)
[A] doubles, Rate doubles → Order = 1

Finding order with respect to B:
Compare experiments 1 and 3 (A constant)
[B] doubles, Rate quadruples (×4) → Order = 2

Rate Equation: Rate = k[A][B]²
Overall order: 1 + 2 = 3

Units of Rate Constant

Quick Formula:
For overall order n: k has units of mol¹⁻ⁿ dm³ⁿ⁻³ s⁻¹

• Order 0: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
• Order 1: s⁻¹
• Order 2: mol⁻¹ dm³ s⁻¹
• Order 3: mol⁻² dm⁶ s⁻¹
AQA note: Always use mol dm⁻³ (not M or mol/L)

Rate-Determining Step (RDS)

Rate-Determining Step
The slowest step in a reaction mechanism.
AQA Mark Point: Must state it is the "slowest step"
Key Rule: Only species that react in or before the RDS appear in the rate equation
Golden Rules for Mechanisms:
1. Species in rate equation must appear in or before the RDS
2. Orders = number of moles of each species up to and including RDS
3. Catalysts appear in mechanism but not in overall equation
4. Species appearing after the RDS do NOT affect the rate equation
Example: Linking Rate Equation to Mechanism
Given Rate Equation: Rate = k[NO]²[H₂]

Proposed Mechanism:
Step 1: 2NO → N₂O₂ (fast equilibrium)
Step 2: N₂O₂ + H₂ → N₂O + H₂O (slow - RDS)
Step 3: N₂O + H₂ → N₂ + H₂O (fast)

Validation:
✓ In RDS: 1 × N₂O₂ and 1 × H₂
✓ N₂O₂ comes from 2NO in step 1
✓ Total: 2NO and 1H₂ matches rate equation
✓ Overall: 2NO + 2H₂ → N₂ + 2H₂O ✓

The Arrhenius Equation

k = Ae⁻ᴱᵃ/ᴿᵀ

ln k = ln A - Ea/RT
The Arrhenius Equation - Key Components
k = rate constant (units depend on overall order)
A = pre-exponential factor or frequency factor (same units as k)
e = mathematical constant (2.718...)
Ea = activation energy (J mol⁻¹ - watch units!)
R = gas constant = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
T = absolute temperature (K)
AQA: Must use natural log (ln) not log₁₀
Common Calculator Errors:
• Forgetting to convert °C to K (add 273)
• Using Ea in kJ instead of J
• Using log₁₀ instead of natural log (ln)
• Forgetting the negative sign

Arrhenius Plots

Plot ln k (y-axis) vs 1/T (x-axis):
• Gradient = -Ea/R
• y-intercept = ln A
• Straight line proves first-order kinetics
Worked Example: Calculating Activation Energy
Given:
• k = 2.5 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹ at 300 K
• k = 5.0 × 10⁻² s⁻¹ at 310 K
Find: Activation energy

Solution:
Using: ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R) × (1/T₁ - 1/T₂)

Step 1: Calculate ln(k₂/k₁)
ln(5.0×10⁻²/2.5×10⁻³) = ln(20) = 2.996

Step 2: Calculate temperature term
1/T₁ - 1/T₂ = 1/300 - 1/310
= 0.003333 - 0.003226 = 1.075 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹

Step 3: Solve for Ea
2.996 = (Ea/8.314) × 1.075 × 10⁻⁴
Ea = (2.996 × 8.314) / (1.075 × 10⁻⁴)
Ea = 231,700 J mol⁻¹ = 232 kJ mol⁻¹
✓ Verified: Reasonable Ea value for typical reactions (50-250 kJ mol⁻¹)

Effect of Temperature on Rate

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution:
• As temperature increases, the distribution curve flattens and shifts right
• More molecules have E ≥ Ea at higher temperatures
• Small temperature increase → large rate increase (exponential relationship)
• Rule of thumb: 10°C rise approximately doubles the rate

Practice Questions

Thermodynamics Practice

Q1: Born-Haber Cycle Calculation

Calculate the lattice enthalpy of formation for KBr using:

  • • ΔH°f(KBr) = -394 kJ mol⁻¹
  • • Atomisation of K = +89 kJ mol⁻¹
  • • Atomisation of ½Br₂(l) = +112 kJ mol⁻¹
  • • IE₁(K) = +419 kJ mol⁻¹
  • • EA₁(Br) = -325 kJ mol⁻¹
Solution:
Using: ΔH°f = ΔH°at(K) + ΔH°at(Br) + IE₁(K) + EA₁(Br) + ΔH°latt

-394 = 89 + 112 + 419 + (-325) + ΔH°latt
-394 = 295 + ΔH°latt
ΔH°latt = -689 kJ mol⁻¹

✓ Check: Negative value confirms exothermic lattice formation
Q2: Entropy & Feasibility

For the reaction: CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)

ΔH = +178 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔS = +160 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

Calculate the minimum temperature for feasibility.

Solution:
At the boundary of feasibility: ΔG = 0
0 = ΔH - TΔS
T = ΔH/ΔS

Convert units: ΔS = 0.160 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
T = 178/0.160 = 1113 K

Answer: Reaction becomes feasible above 1113 K (840°C)
Explanation: High temperature needed to overcome positive ΔH

Rate Equations Practice

Q3: SN1 Reaction

(CH₃)₃CCl + OH⁻ → (CH₃)₃COH + Cl⁻

Experiment [(CH₃)₃CCl] / mol dm⁻³ [OH⁻] / mol dm⁻³ Initial rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
1 0.015 0.01 2.1 × 10⁻⁴
2 0.03 0.01 4.2 × 10⁻⁴
3 0.03 0.02 4.2 × 10⁻⁴
Order with respect to (CH₃)₃CCl:
Compare exp 1 & 2: conc. doubles, rate doubles → Order = 1

Order with respect to OH⁻:
Compare exp 2 & 3: conc. doubles, rate unchanged → Order = 0

Rate equation: Rate = k[(CH₃)₃CCl]
Overall order: 1

Calculate k:
k = Rate/[(CH₃)₃CCl] = (2.1 × 10⁻⁴)/0.015 = 0.014 s⁻¹

Mechanism insight: SN1 mechanism - rate depends only on leaving group departure
Q4: NO and O₂ Reaction

2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g)

Experiment [NO] / mol dm⁻³ [O₂] / mol dm⁻³ Initial rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
1 0.2 0.3 1.2 × 10⁻⁴
2 0.2 0.6 1.2 × 10⁻⁴
3 0.4 0.3 4.8 × 10⁻⁴
Order with respect to O₂:
Compare exp 1 & 2: [O₂] doubles, rate unchanged → Order = 0

Order with respect to NO:
Compare exp 1 & 3: [NO] doubles, rate × 4 → Order = 2

Rate equation: Rate = k[NO]²

Calculate k:
k = Rate/[NO]² = (1.2 × 10⁻⁴)/(0.2)² = 3 × 10⁻³ mol⁻¹ dm³ s⁻¹

RDS insight: Contains 2 moles of NO, no O₂ in RDS

Mixed Practice Questions

Challenge: Linking Thermodynamics & Kinetics

A reaction has ΔG = -50 kJ mol⁻¹ at 298 K but doesn't occur at room temperature. Explain why.

Answer:
• ΔG < 0 means the reaction is thermodynamically feasible
• However, feasibility ≠ reaction will occur
• The activation energy must be very high
• At room temperature, very few molecules have E ≥ Ea
• Therefore, the rate is negligibly slow despite being feasible

AQA Mark Points:
✓ Distinguish between thermodynamic feasibility and kinetic factors
✓ Mention activation energy as the kinetic barrier
✓ State that feasible reactions may not occur if rate is too slow
